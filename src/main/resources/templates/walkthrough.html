Walkthrough Page

Whether you are brand new to cryptography, or an old hand looking to pick up a few new tricks, you are in the right spot. Here, you will find pages on all of the cipher systems in use on this site, as well as decryption techniques to help solving the challenges. First, a few terms you will see in use, either on this site or in your further explorations of cryptography.

Cryptography: any means by which the contents of a message are hidden from unintended viewers. This can include scrambling the message, running each letter through some algorithm to disguise it, replacing words with other preselected words, or even hiding the existence of a message entirely.

Plaintext: the message being sent, written out in plain english (or whatever language you are communicating in) with no cryptography to hide it. Also referred to as "cleartext."

Ciphertext: the encoded or enciphered message to be sent. This will usually look like a random jumble of words or letters. It is usually considered best practice to remove all punctuation, spacing, and line breaks, and to spell out numbers (1, 2, 3) as words (one, two, three) so they don't stand out. We have challenges in both formats, to highlight the differences.

Code: cryptography that operates on the information level of a message. A word, phrase, or even full sentence will be mapped to another word, phrase, or symbol, without there necessarily being any relation between them. In World War II, the phrase "Jean has a long mustache," was broadcast to the french resistance on D-Day to signal the beginning of the allied invasion. This is an example of a code. The phrase has no correlation whatsoever with the message being sent. There is no algorithm that can extract "Allies landing at Normandy" from the code phrase. It works because both parties, the sender and receiver, had already agreed on a list of phrases and their meanings. Any code requires this pre-meeting and agreement. Longer codes will require a /codebook/, a list of words and their meaning in context of the code. Hiding a message through use of a code is referred to as "encoding."

We do not cover code making on this site, but focus purely on /ciphers/.

Cipher: cryprography that operates on the character level of a message. Ciphers use some scheme or algorithm to mask individual letters and numbers of a message, either by replacing the individual characters with different ones, scramble the order that they were sent in, or replace each letter with an arbitrary symbol, picked in advance. Because ciphers are based on algorithms, patterns tend to emerge, and most can be broken through careful thought and concentrated attack. Hiding a message through the use of a cipher is known as "encryption" or "encipherment."

Steganography: a method of cryptography in which a message is hidden from view in some way. This can mean anything from shrinking a message down until you need a microscope to see it to writing in invisible ink. There is even a story of a greek noble sending a message by shaving his servants head and tattooing the message on his bare scalp, then waiting for his hair to regrow. When the message was hidden, the servant was sent on a routine errand, and the intended recipient shaved the servants head and read the message. Now, we tend to treat people a little better than that, so the preferred method of hiding message is to use digital noise in some way, such as the least important bit in an image file.

Steganography is another field we will not be covering on this site, but you are encouraged to research it online elsewhere.

Key: the piece of knowledge needed to decrypt a message. This can be anything from a number to a word to an entire codebook. They key is the knowledge that must be kept hidden. In a well contructed cipher, even if your adversary knows what technique you used to encrypt a message, they will be powerless to decrypt it without the key. Because of this, the number of possible keys is one measure of how secure a cipher system is. Later, you will learn about the Caesar shift cipher, a method that has only 26 possible keys. This is very weak compared to modern digital ciphers, which often have trillions and trillions of possible keys.

Cryptanalysis: any method used to figure out the contents of an encrypted message without having knowledge of the key beforehand. Also refered to as "breaking" or "cracking" a code or cipher. Cryptanalysis can require a mix of math, linguistics, logic, pattern recognition, and often some lucky guesswork, but we try to provide a gentle introduction here.
