Rail Fence Cipher

The Rail Fence Cipher is one of the earliest examples of a transposition cipher, as opposed to a substitution cipher, which we have been covered so far. In a transposition cipher, the letters remain the same, but the order is scrambled according to some predetermined pattern, rendering the message unreadable. Let's see this in action.

We'll begin with the message "Fences keep good secrets". Write the message on alternating lines, with the first letter on the top line, the second on the bottom line, third on the top line, and so on.
F N E K E G O S C E S
E C S E P O D E R T

Now we simply read the message across to gain the ciphertext, which in this case is
F N E K E G O S C E S E C S E P O D E R T

Notice that the ciphertext is made up of the same letters as the plaintext. This is the key difference between a substitution cipher and a transposition cipher. It also means the letter frequency does not change from regular english. If you are ever trying to crack a cipher, and your frequency analysis very closely matches natural english, there is a decent chance you are looking at a transposition cipher.

If we wanted to, we could use 3 lines, instead of 2, as seen here:
F   E   E   O   C   S
E C S E P O D E R T
N   K   G   S   E
which leads to a new ciphertext of
F E E O C S E C S E P O D E R T N K G S E

As long as the message is long enough, we can use as many lines as we want. Unfortunately, no method leads to very strong security, as there are few enough possibilities that an attacker can try them all by hand, or write a very simple computer program to test all possible options.
